## Potential Actions

### Featured: Balboa Reservoir Building A — Board Vote April 7

**What's happening.** The Board of Supervisors votes Tuesday on the ground lease and $29M loan for Building A at Balboa Reservoir (105 Wisteria Lane). This is 158 units of 100% affordable rental housing for very-low and low-income households — the first building to break ground in a development that's been in planning for years.

**Why you'd care.** This is one of the largest affordable housing projects touching D4. The site sits at the district's southern edge near City College. How fast this moves affects whether the rest of Balboa Reservoir follows.

**What you can do.** Public comment is open at the Board meeting, Tuesday April 7, 2:00 PM, City Hall. You can also email the full Board at board.of.supervisors@sfgov.org with "Item 260242" in the subject line. A two-minute email counts.

## Housing Pipeline

The big story at 2700-2750 Sloat Blvd continues: a 682-unit, 100% affordable project has been resubmitted under State Density Bonus, replacing two earlier withdrawn proposals (a 680-unit tower and a 650-unit AB 2011 project). Separately, 3601 Lawton St (46 units, 8 stories, replacing an auto shop) received its approval letter. And 1234 Great Highway (199 affordable senior units with adult day health center) is now listed as "on hold." The educator housing project at 1351 42nd Ave (135 units, 100% affordable) shows as closed.

## Permits

Two notable filings: a $465K two-story rear extension at 1626 22nd Ave adding bedrooms and baths, and a garage-to-living-space conversion at 2454 30th Ave (723 sf, adding bedroom, office, family room). Storefront repairs at 2514-2518 Taraval — a car drove into the building, causing moderate damage; windows and glass doors being replaced under a notice of violation. The rest of the week's permits are reroofing (9 filings), kitchen/bath remodels, and two solar installations.

## Citywide

**John Elberling dies at 79.** The TODCO founder and SoMa affordable housing advocate shaped San Francisco development politics for decades. Coverage in 48 Hills and Mission Local.

**Rod Diridon dies at 87.** The Bay Area transit leader and high-speed rail champion, namesake of San Jose's Diridon Station.

**Sacks wants his GrowSF money back.** Trump AI czar David Sacks donated to the Lurie-allied group; now he's asking for a refund. GrowSF says the money is already spent.

**Asian Law Caucus at the Supreme Court.** The SF-based civil rights group is at the center of the birthright citizenship case before SCOTUS.

**Ethics Commission / Lurie-Trump records.** The Sunshine Ordinance Task Force found the mayor violated open-records law regarding phone records with Trump. The Ethics Commission takes it up next. The mayor's office says there are no more documents.

**Surveillance expansion at the Board.** Multiple surveillance technology policies heading to votes this week — police location tracking, airport monitoring, DPW drones with license plate readers. These set precedent for how the city deploys tracking technology.

**Land Use Committee — 14 landmark designations Monday.** All in D8/D9 (Castro, Mission, Cole Valley). Includes the Bob Ross House, SF AIDS Foundation building, Castro Rock Steam Baths, and 11 others. Also on the docket: easing Institutional Master Plan requirements for universities (relevant to UCSF and other D4-adjacent institutions) and allowing up to 8-room hotels in RH-2/RH-3 zones.

**Police overtime: $34M reallocation.** The Board votes on shifting $34.4M from regular salaries to overtime in the police budget — a required accounting move, but the overtime reliance is notable.

**Cannabis cafes ordinance introduced.** Would let existing cannabis retailers add on-premises consumption. First-year eligibility limited to current storefront permit holders.

**Climate Action Plan update.** Environment Code amendments updating the city's climate goals and departmental responsibilities — first reading at the Board.

**Budget outlook improving.** The city's fiscal picture is getting better, though the Lurie CEO tax proposal and its revenue implications remain in play.
