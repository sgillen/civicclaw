## The Fielder Era Begins

Fielder came into office as the clearest progressive mandate on the Board — winning by a wider margin than any other supervisor. But the rest of the Board swung moderate in the same election, leaving her as the left-most voice on an 7-4 (or sometimes 8-3) moderate-majority Board.

Her first year defined a pattern: propose progressive legislation, lose on the big votes, claim smaller wins on process and oversight. She pushed a law to end the city's 90-day limit for homeless family shelter stays — a real policy change affecting Mission families who were being cycled back onto the street. She called for an investigation into a $5.9 million sole-source permitting contract signed by Lurie's office. And she consistently voted against the mayor's housing agenda when she thought it lacked displacement protections.

In December, SF Standard called her first major legislative passage "actually a huge loss" — the resolution passed, but by the time it got through committee, it had been watered down from binding requirements to recommendations.

**Committees:** Fielder chairs the Government Audit and Oversight Committee and sits on Budget and Appropriations.

## Housing: The Pipeline Turns On

After years of approvals gathering dust, 2025 was the year D9 affordable housing started moving from paper to dirt.

**Casa Adelante — 1515 South Van Ness** broke ground in April 2025: 168 units, 100% affordable, targeting low-income families including formerly homeless households and people living with HIV. Built by MEDA and Chinatown CDC. Expected completion 2027. This is the Mission's largest affordable project in 20 years.

**La Maravilla — 1979 Mission St** (Phase 1: 134 units of the eventual ~400-unit project) began site preparation in December 2025, with construction starting early 2026. When complete, La Maravilla will be the single largest affordable housing development the Mission has ever seen.

**2675 Folsom St** received its planning approval for 237 affordable senior housing units (100% affordable), one of the biggest approvals of the year.

**3333 Mission St** (70 affordable senior units, SB 35) got approved in an amended form — the design was revised to shift density between two buildings on the site, maintaining the same unit count.

**The 2588 Mission controversy** was the year's most heated housing fight. Developer Hawk Ling Lou's 181-unit market-rate building (with ~20% affordable) survived a challenge at Planning Commission in a 4-3 vote in May. Opponents — including longtime Mission residents and the community group that coined "La Muerte de la Misión" — argued the project would accelerate displacement of Latinx residents. Supporters countered that blocking housing wouldn't slow displacement and would worsen the overall shortage.

**The math:** D9 has approximately 1,100+ housing units in the streamlined pipeline across roughly 20 projects. About 60% of the pipeline units are 100% affordable — far more than most districts. But the approval-to-construction gap remains brutal: only 2% of citywide streamlined projects have broken ground.

## Citywide Upzoning: The Family Zoning Vote

The biggest land use fight of 2025 was the mayor's plan to upzone much of western and northern San Francisco — the so-called "family zoning" plan. It passed the Board 7-4 in December.

Fielder voted no. Her argument: the plan allows more market-rate density in historically protected neighborhoods without requiring meaningful affordable housing percentages or anti-displacement protections in neighborhoods like the Mission and Portola that have already experienced decades of displacement. The Mission was largely carved out of the upzoning (it already has higher zoning), but Fielder's concern was about the precedent and the missing protections.

The debate revealed the fundamental tension in D9's relationship with housing policy: the Mission is simultaneously the neighborhood that needs the most affordable housing and the one most traumatized by market-rate development.

## Waymo Killed KitKat

In October, a Waymo autonomous vehicle struck and killed KitKat, a cat that lived at a bodega in the Mission near Randa's Market. It became the most San Francisco news story of the year.

Fielder held a "Justice for KitKat" rally, then introduced a resolution calling on the state legislature to give cities the power to regulate (or ban) autonomous vehicles. She also began working with Sacramento legislators on state-level AV regulation.

The response was polarized: some saw it as a genuine safety concern (AVs had been involved in multiple incidents with pedestrians and animals), others saw it as political theater. SF Standard's headline: "Never let a dead cat go to waste."

Regardless of the optics, the resolution moved through committee and Fielder got serious attention on AV regulation from state legislators — not bad for a freshman supervisor working from a dead cat.

## Lobbying

**Teamster Joint Council 7** was the year's dominant D9 lobbying presence — roughly 25+ contacts with Fielder's office across the year, spanning transportation (including a parcel tax), planning and building permits, public works, and economic development. The Teamsters monitor/support pattern was the most persistent of any lobbyist-supervisor pairing in the city.

**Golden City FC** (the proposed soccer stadium project) logged multiple contacts in May-June, seeking Fielder's support on planning and building permits.

**SF Chamber of Commerce** (David Harrison) opposed Fielder on at least one planning item in December (likely related to the upzoning vote).

**Frontier Group LLC** sought approval on a planning matter in November.

**Northern California Carpenters Regional Council** lobbied Fielder to delay action on a planning item in June — notable because carpenters and teamsters are usually aligned, but this suggests internal labor disagreement on specific projects.

## Evictions

D9 evictions in 2025 tracked at roughly 120-150 notices for the year, with the majority being non-payment and nuisance evictions. No Ellis Act withdrawal activity — a significant absence in a district where Ellis Act evictions were once a primary displacement mechanism.

The geographic concentration remained the same: the Mission absorbed roughly 55% of all D9 evictions, Bernal Heights ~35%, Portola ~10%.

**One pattern to watch:** non-payment evictions trended upward in Q3-Q4 2025, which can be an early indicator of rent affordability stress rather than traditional displacement tactics. The cluster of non-payment notices on the 900 block of Ellsworth Street (Bernal Heights) in early 2026 may be connected to a single building.

## 311 Pulse

The Mission's 311 signature remained graffiti, street cleaning, and encampment reports as the three dominant categories. Encampment reports showed the most geographic concentration — clustered along the 16th St corridor, Mission St from 16th to 24th, and the Folsom/Harrison corridors in the eastern Mission.

Property damage reports spiked periodically around the Valencia/Mission corridor.

## The Year in Context

2025 was Jackie Fielder's freshman year, and it played out as a preview of the tensions that would define D9 politics going forward: a progressive supervisor fighting for tenant protections and affordable housing in a district where market-rate development is both feared and needed, on a board that has shifted firmly toward the mayor's moderate agenda.

The affordable housing starts were genuinely historic — 1515 South Van Ness and 1979 Mission St alone represent 560+ affordable units moving toward construction. But the market-rate pipeline also advanced (2588 Mission, and more in the queue), and the community's anxiety about displacement didn't diminish.

Fielder's oversight work — the shelter stay legislation, the contract probe, the Waymo push — showed a supervisor who understood her role as a minority-bloc voice: use the tools you have (hearings, resolutions, public pressure) when you can't win the vote count.
