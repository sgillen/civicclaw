## WHAT THESE OFFICES ACTUALLY DO

### Board of Supervisors
11-member legislative body. Passes laws, approves the city budget (~$16B/yr), confirms mayoral appointments, holds hearings. Each supervisor represents ~75,000 residents. A swing vote on the 11-member board can determine whether housing gets built, how police are funded, whether transit projects move forward. This is where SF politics actually happens.

### Assessor-Recorder — **Joaquín Torres** (running for reelection)
Sets assessed value of every property → determines property tax bills. Also records deeds, liens, documents. Sounds boring. It's not. This is where Prop 13 inheritance loopholes get exploited or caught, where corporate shell structures evade reassessment, where commercial landlords quietly underpay. Torres has pushed to close some of those gaps. If you care about who actually pays taxes in SF, this matters.

### Public Defender — **Mano Raju** (running for reelection)
Represents people who can't afford a lawyer in criminal cases — constitutional right. In SF this is politically charged: the PD's office often clashes with DA and police on drug charges, gang injunctions, cash bail, immigration consequences. Raju has been an aggressive systemic reformer. Elected PD is unusual nationally; SF takes it seriously.

### Superior Court Judges
Hear criminal cases, family court, civil disputes, landlord-tenant cases. Almost always run unopposed as incumbents — real opportunity only when seats are open. Often the most consequential race nobody thinks about.

### Board of Education
Sets SFUSD goals, approves curriculum and budget, appoints superintendent. Recently turbulent after recall of progressive board members in 2022. Now a proxy battle between reform/accountability faction vs. traditional progressive education coalition.

### BART Director
Oversees Bay Area Rapid Transit board. SF has 2 director seats. Rarely contested but relevant for anyone who cares about transit reliability and the planned BART extensions.

## DISTRICT 2 — SPECIAL ELECTION (June 2)

**Background:** D2 is the Marina, Cow Hollow, Pacific Heights — wealthy, moderate-to-conservative by SF standards. Seat vacated when Catherine Stefani went to State Assembly in 2024.

### Stephen Sherrill (incumbent, appointed by Mayor Breed)
- Age 39, Yale grad, homeowner in D2 since 2015
- Transportation: drives, transit, bikes
- Prior: senior policy advisor Bloomberg admin NYC; led SF Mayor's Office of Innovation
- Top issue: fentanyl crisis — enforcement + treatment + recovery beds
- **Endorsements:** Mayor Lurie, GrowSF, SF Police Officers Association, Nor Cal Carpenters Union
- **Narrative:** The technocratic moderate. Aligned with Lurie. Strong on public safety messaging. Good governance credentials. Least ideologically charged candidate in either race.

### Lori Brooke
- Age 62, homeowner D2 for 31 years, President of Cow Hollow Association
- Transportation: drives and walks
- Top issue: safe and clean streets — visible enforcement, more foot patrols
- **Endorsements:** Former Supervisor Michela Alioto-Pier, former Senator Quentin Kopp, former Senator Mark Leno, retired SFPD Deputy Chief Anne Mannix
- **Narrative:** The community institution candidate. Deep local roots. Law-and-order emphasis. More conservative than Sherrill on policing. Less policy apparatus, more neighborhood credibility.

### Jeremy Kirshner
- Less publicly prominent than the other two. Limited coverage found.

**The real dynamic:** Sherrill has the mayor's blessing and institutional support. Brooke has the old-guard neighborhood establishment. Both are moderate/right of center by SF standards. Neither is running on housing or transit in any meaningful way — D2 is not a YIMBY district.

## DISTRICT 4 — SPECIAL ELECTION (June 2)

**Background:** D4 is the Outer Sunset — primarily Chinese-American, working/middle class homeowners, moderate-conservative by SF standards. Historically underrepresented in city hall. The last year has been chaos: Supervisor Engardio recalled in Sept 2025 over Great Highway (Prop K), botched first appointment (Beya Alcaraz, music teacher, resigned), then Alan Wong appointed Nov 30. Now 5-way race.

**The Great Highway fault line:** Prop K permanently closed the Great Highway and turned it into a park. Passed citywide, but D4 voted against it — it's their route, their lifeline. This issue drove the recall and defines the race.

### Alan Wong (incumbent, appointed by Mayor Lurie)
- Born and raised in D4
- Transportation: unknown
- Prior: legislative aide to former Supervisor Gordon Mar (4 years), CCSF Board of Trustees
- Top issue: district representation, continuity
- **Money:** Raised $17,327 (3rd of 5). Key donors: Mayor Lurie ($500), mayor's chief of staff ($250), mayor's liaison to BOS ($250), mayor's public affairs director ($500), Sheriff Miyamoto ($250), Stop Crime SF founder ($500), D5 Supervisor Mahmood ($500), Mark Leno ($500)
- **Endorsements:** GrowSF
- **Public financing:** No
- **Narrative:** The Lurie establishment pick. Institutional support but thin grassroots. Turned against labor (AFT 2121) over CCSF chancellor fight. Former boss Gordon Mar endorsed his opponent Gee — notable.

### Natalie Gee
- Age 40, renter in D4 since 2021, born elsewhere
- Job: Chief of staff to D10 Supervisor Shamann Walton (progressive); part-time Pilates instructor
- Transportation: drives, walks, Muni
- Top issue: affordability — "it's too expensive to live"
- **Money:** Raised $67,250 — more than all other candidates combined. Cash on hand $55k. Qualifies for public financing (6:1 match on donations ≤$150 from SF voters = massive multiplier)
- **Endorsements:** IFPTE Local 21 (sole endorsement), SEIU 1021, IUEC Local 8, AFT 2121, Supervisors Melgar/Chan/Walton, former Supervisor Gordon Mar
- **Narrative:** The progressive labor candidate. Strong fundraising, strong institutional progressive support. Her association with Walton (Lurie critic) made her an unlikely Lurie appointment. Renter in a homeowner district — potential liability. Best organized campaign in the race.

### Albert Chow
- Age 58, homeowner in D4 since 1978, UC Berkeley grad
- Job: Owner of Great Wall Hardware; President of People of Parkside Sunset
- Transportation: drives and walks
- Top issue: "real representation" — anti-Engardio recall organizer, explicitly framing this as reclaiming D4's voice
- **Money:** Raised $6,151. No public financing.
- **Endorsements:** Retired SFPD Commander Richard Corriea, retired SFPD Commander Peter Walsh
- **Narrative:** The community insurgent. Led the Engardio recall. Most authentically district-rooted candidate — 48 years in D4. Law-and-order lean. Small business credibility. Crowd favorite at the forum alongside Gee. Limited institutional support but strong grassroots brand.

### David Lee
- Job: Executive Director, Chinese American Voter Education Committee; SF State professor
- Transportation: unknown
- Prior: ran for D1 Supervisor three times; ran for State Assembly
- **Money:** Raised $19,950, $18,584 cash on hand (2nd of 5)
- **Narrative:** The perennial candidate. Has the Chinese-American institutional network via CAVEC. Framing: fiscal accountability, asks "tough questions." Serial candidate history is both a credential and a liability. More establishment than Chow, less progressive than Gee.

### Jeremy Greco
- Longtime Sunset resident, campus coordinator at Presidio Hill School, former worker-owner of Other Avenues grocery co-op
- **Money:** Reported $0 in contributions
- **Notable position:** Pro-Sunset Dunes (the only candidate who doesn't want to close the park/reopen the highway as park)
- Did not participate in the March candidate forum
- **Narrative:** The wildcard. No money, no institutional support, skipped the forum. But ideologically distinct — the only candidate aligned with keeping Prop K. Probably not a factor but worth tracking.

## KEY FAULT LINES (both races)

**Lurie orbit vs. independent:** Wong and Sherrill are both Lurie-appointed incumbents with his explicit support. Gee and Brooke are running against that alignment (Gee explicitly; Brooke implicitly through old-guard endorsements).

**Labor vs. moderate:** Gee has the progressive labor stack. Wong has the mayor and law-and-order donors. This is the classic SF progressive vs. moderate split playing out in an unusual district (Sunset, not the Mission).

**The Great Highway:** The defining local issue in D4. Most candidates want it reopened (or are strategically vague). Greco is the outlier. Wong is trying to thread the needle — appointed by Lurie who signed off on Prop K, but needs D4 votes.

**Housing:** Barely discussed in D4 so far. The district is homeowner-dominant and skeptical of upzoning. Gee's "affordability" framing is renter-friendly but vague on new construction. This is where your pro-housing lens produces real signal — none of these candidates are running on building more housing in the Sunset.

## MEASURES

### Prop A — Earthquake Safety and Emergency Response Bond
Funds seismic upgrades, emergency infrastructure. Requires 2/3 vote. **GrowSF: Yes. SF Dems: Yes.** Essentially noncontroversial — will pass.

### Prop B — Charter Amendment: Lifetime Term Limits for Mayor and Supervisors
Prevents termed-out officials from ever returning to the same office. **GrowSF: Yes. SF Dems: Yes.** Reform measure, bipartisan support.

### Prop C — Business Tax Cuts
Reduces taxes on certain businesses. **GrowSF: Yes.** Pro-business/moderate coalition. Will be contested by progressive/labor side.

### Prop D — CEO Pay Ratio Tax
Increases business taxes based on ratio of top exec pay to median employee pay. **GrowSF: No.** Progressive measure. Classic labor vs. tech/business fault line.
