## D5 This Week

### The real housing fight this week is about the rules, not a single project

Monday’s Land Use agenda is more important than it looks. One Oak is a familiar San Francisco argument — can a big housing project get a little more envelope in exchange for actually moving forward — but the bigger item is SB 79 implementation. That one is about whether SF treats transit-oriented upzoning as a production tool or as a legal obligation to be narrowed with caveats. If you care about whether D5 and adjacent transit corridors can absorb more housing, this is one of the more consequential hearings in weeks.

### Page Street is getting another safety turn of the ratchet

SFMTA’s April 17 engineering hearing proposes a **15 mph speed limit on Page between Stanyan and Franklin**. That stretch matters because Page is already one of the city’s bike-priority corridors, and the change would make the legal street design better match how it’s supposed to function: lower-speed, neighborhood-scale, bike-heavy, park-adjacent movement instead of a pseudo-through-route. It still needs Board approval, but it’s a tangible D5 street-safety item with a clear pedestrian/cyclist upside.

### Preservation keeps advancing one property at a time

The Board’s Tuesday agenda includes a dense stack of landmark designations, and two are squarely in your extended geography: **1451 Masonic** and **1757 Waller**. None of these votes individually stops a big housing project tomorrow, but the cumulative pattern still matters. D5 keeps seeing more preservation layers added while the city is simultaneously trying to explain why housing production remains slow and fragile.

### The housing pipeline is still splitting into “affordable moves” and “market-rate stalls”

The cleanest positive signal is **880 McAllister**, a 100% affordable senior project that now shows **approval letter issued** status. That fits the broader city pattern from the build-timelines reference: affordable projects with assembled financing can move from entitlement to permit much faster than market-rate housing. The darker side of the same dataset is still there too: **550 O’Farrell is on hold**, and older Tenderloin projects at **39 Taylor, 819 Ellis, and 841 Polk** remain cancelled. The story is less “nothing gets approved” than “approval is not the bottleneck once money gets hard.”

## Citywide

### Transit and street money is moving even while service politics stay murky

The SFCTA Board on Tuesday is set to allocate **$9.8 million in Prop L funds**, including money for **Powell Street improvements, 22nd Street ADA access improvements, and the Mission Bay ferry landing**, while also taking up the county framework for the next **OBAG** cycle. That’s useful evidence that transportation capital funding is still moving through the system even while the harder fights — operating budgets, service quality, and street-space tradeoffs — stay politically sticky.

### SFMTA is still doing incremental street governance, not dramatic reform

Last week’s SFMTA Board agenda was mostly incremental, but the standout was the **FY 26-27 / 27-28 Muni Service Equity Strategy Report**, alongside a curbside EV charging amendment and routine traffic modifications. The broad read is that the agency is still in planning-and-adjustment mode rather than announcing a big service or street-design swing. That matters because D5’s everyday transit experience depends less on one splashy project than on whether this slow machinery actually compounds into better reliability and safer corridors.

### Ethics and election infrastructure are starting to wake up for 2026

The Ethics Commission’s visible signal this week was election plumbing, not scandal: it certified publicly financed Board candidates in **Districts 8 and 10** and launched updated campaign-finance dashboards ahead of the 2026 cycle. That’s not a D5 story yet, but it’s a reminder that the next round of supervisor politics is already being scaffolded, and the money/information rails are turning on early.

### Journalism is mostly politics and city narrative, not a single D5 scoop

Local coverage this week leaned more citywide than neighborhood-specific. The piece most worth clocking is Mission Local’s report on the new **Chakrabarti poll against Wiener**, because it says the congressional race is tightening into a real contest instead of a ceremonial glide path. That’s not municipal government exactly, but it matters for the broader ideological map of SF politics: urbanist state-preemption energy versus a more insurgent local-left challenge.
